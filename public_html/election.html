Calvinist theology is often identified by "The Five Points of Calvinism."  Of them, the point most argued and debated among believers in Christ is the one of  Unconditional Election,  a doctrine that asserts that God's choice from eternity of those whom He will bring to Himself is not based on foreseen virtue, merit, or faith in those individuals.  Rather, it is based unconditionally on God's sovereignty and His grace alone.  This, Calvinists base largely on Romans 9:10-16.  Essentially, they hold that when God chooses this individual or that (while just as arbitrarily rejecting others),  you and I have no choice in accepting or rejecting God.  Therefore, they say, "we have nothing in our relationship with God about which we can boast."  I agree.  I have nothing in my relationship with God about which I can boast, but  not  for the same reasons Calvinists raise.

In the disquisition that follows we will look at several Scriptural points we believe show that God's giving man the freedom to choose or reject Him actually proves God to be more powerful and more sovereign than the Calvinist God who essentially chooses only automatons.

An Omniscient and Omnipotent God
There are attributes of God we all need to consider first when examining the issues of choice and boasting. God is both omniscient and omnipotent.  Even though Calvinists argue that in regards to whom God would choose to save (His "elect"), He still knew before the creation of the heavens and the earth, who would  and would not  accept Him through His son, Jesus.  Such is omniscience.  To refresh our memories, here are the definitions of  omniscient  and  omnipotent.

Predestination springs from omnipotence – having all power so as to arrange everything beforehand.  Foreknowledge, on the other hand, comes from omniscience – having all knowledge to know what will happen beforehand.   Predestination before foreknowledge  actually requires less sovereign power than does  foreknowledge before predestination.   Those who believe the latter, worship a much more powerful God than those who believe the former simply because it takes a more powerful God to maintain sovereignty in a world where man lives with a God-given ability to accept or reject Him.

 alvinists often argue that men claiming free will and choosing to accept God's gracious salvation have reason to boast about their salvation and, therefore, do not have a genuinely humble attitude toward God and His grace.  Do those who claim that  "God chose  THEM"  sound more humble?

Consider the thief on the cross next to Christ.  He had  nothing  about which to boast, save his acceptance of Christ's offer of salvation.  But even his acceptance of that could never be construed as something about which he could boast – except, perhaps, in one sense – he could boast that Christ died for him.

Exercising God-given free will and accepting salvation gives one NOTHING about which to boast. The gift was  offered  to ALL men, equally, and not to  just those who accept it.  Moreover, simply accepting it in no way makes one worthy of heaven or even more noble than those who refuse it. God's graciousness is greater precisely because it is offered to ALL men (1 John 2:2).  Whatever one's sins, it will be the refusal of  that gift  and the price Christ paid on the cross, that will ultimately condemn one's soul to hell.

Freewill man, with his God-given ability to accept or reject salvation in no way reduces the sovereignty of God.  If anything, accepting God's gracious gift only comes from one who first, recognizes and acknowledges that  he is completely and totally unworthy of the gift,  and  like every man,  is powerless to ever earn or merit the gift at all.  Any other attitude toward salvation totally negates and nullifies the individual's response and acceptance since any other attitude is  salvation by works  or merit,  and not of grace.

Is Order Important to God?
The apostle Paul was inspired of God to write the following in 1 Corinthians 14:40.

Let all things be done decently and in order.
That passage should be an obvious answer to the question (written the same way in some 21 translations), but, where possible, we should always seek other passages that affirm the Truth of God in His Word.  Consider now the apostle Paul's account in 1 Thessalonians 4:15-17 of the Rapture.

15   For this we say unto you by the word of the Lord, that we
which are alive and remain unto the coming of the Lord
shall not prevent [precede] them which are asleep.
16   For the Lord himself shall descend from heaven with a shout,
with the voice of the archangel, and with the trump of God:
and the dead in Christ shall rise first:
17   Then we which are alive and remain shall be caught up
together with them in the clouds, to meet the Lord in
the air: and so shall we ever be with the Lord.

We who are alive will NOT precede those believers in Christ who have already passed from this life.  God makes that clear, repeating it twice in those three verses.  Clearly, there is an order of events preceding the end times (as it says in 1 Corinthians 14:33, our God is "not  the author of confusion" [Emphasis added]). 

Look with me now at the beginning of life as we know it.  Consider His creation of the heavens, the earth, and mankind.

From the perspective of creation, the answer to our question,  "Is Order Important to God?"  should also be an obvious,  "Yes,"  for God created the heavens, the earth, and mankind in order.  God created light  before  He created the sun – the generator, as it were, of our solar system's light ! He separated the land and the waters before He had the plants and animals appear therein.  God's order in His creation of the heavens and the earth  is  obvious – read about it in Genesis. 2

Pushing our question about the importance of order still further, we must ask ourselves, is it fair to say that in absence of anything to the contrary in Scripture, that order is also important to God when it comes to His Word?  Consider for a moment the words of the prophet Jeremiah as found in  Jeremiah 9:23-24.

23   Let not the wise boast of his wisdom,
Nor the mighty boast of his power,
Neither let the rich boast of his wealth;
24   Rather, let him that boasts, boast in this:
That he understands and knows Me –
That I am the LORD ... [Emphasis added]
 
Is order important to God in what He says?  In His Word?  Can we, for example, switch the order of the words "understands" and "knows" in verse 24?  Or can we ignore its order?  One of the major points of that passage is that we can boast of  NOTHING.   Were the order switched then some might argue that they can boast in that they know Him – at least from His Word.  But the order is not "knows and understands."  It is just the opposite – "understands and knows" – meaning that we really have NOTHING about which we can boast 3, for who of mankind, save Jesus, can rightly claim that they "understand" AND "know" God?

The Question of Election
So, in the absence of any Scripture declaring or showing that order is unimportant, and in the presence of an ordered, created universe, it seems completely reasonable to say that "Order is important to God."  With that in mind, let us turn now to the question of  election  beginning with Romans 8:28-30.

28   And we know that all things work together for good
to them that love God 4,  to them who are the called
according to his purpose.
29   For whom he did  foreknow, he also did  predestine
to be conformed to the image of his Son, that he
might be the firstborn among many brethren.
30   Moreover whom he did predestine, them he also
called: and whom he called, them he also  justified:
and whom he justified, them he also  glorified.

Note the order of the five critical words found in verses 29 and 30:

1.  Foreknow
2.  Predestine

5.  Glorified 		  – Knowing we would accept Christ as Lord and Savior.
  – To be conformed to the image of Christ.
  – According to His specific purposes for each of our lives.
  – A judicial act releasing one from condemnation.
  – Our new, eternal, glorious minds and bodies.

God did not  casually  put that text together: Order has always been, and still is, important to God.  Consider the last word, "Glorified." 

That word is last  precisely because none of us will be "glorified" until our Lord's return.  So who can reasonably or logically deny the criticality of its placement or order in that list?  Now, consider the first word in that list.  Consider "Foreknowledge."    Foreknowledge precedes predestination,  calling 5,  justification,  and glorification.  Clearly, their order  is  everything here.

Facing the fact that the order of the words, combined with all the scriptures clearly affirming that order  is important to God,  many then try to shift the argument away from that by saying,

"Well, it was not God's knowing beforehand who would accept Him as their God that motivated His predestination and calling of them."
Unfortunately for Calvinists, there remain four other words that are just as significant as "foreknowledge."  They are the four words that immediately precede the word, "foreknow." – "For whom He did  foreknow ..."    Look again at the preceding translations and versions of God's Word.  Those words appear before the word for  foreknowledge  in each of them.  "For those whom He foreknew" seems quite specific.  In fact, even the apostle Peter in his first epistle opens with a statement affirming our election to be just that – foreknown.  In  1 Peter 1:1-2  we read,
1   Peter, an apostle of Jesus Christ, to the strangers scattered throughout

For those preferring the New International Version (©1984) that second verse reads as follows:
2   who have been  chosen  according to the foreknowledge of   God the Father,

One may argue all he wants against the importance of order to God,  but regardless of translation, Peter's words remain quite clear – "Elect" or "Chosen" – both come "according to the foreknowledge  of God."  Foreknowledge, yet again, precedes Election – the encapsulating word for predestination and calling.  It preceds them here, just as it does in Romans, Chapter 8.

Foreknowledge Defined
In the original language, the Greek word translated  foreknow  and  foreknew  is "proginosko" (prog-in-oce'-ko) which means, to know beforehand, that is, foresee, foreknow, or to know before.

Before God created the heavens and the earth He knew that as a completely undeserving sinner, I would nevertheless, by faith, accept Jesus, His Son, Christ as my Savior and Lord.  Knowing that before it ever happened – knowing that first, God then predestined and called (elected) me.

It is true that God does not "foresee" men doing things in the future as we think of foreseeing something.  No, God sees all things in His  unbounded now.  Regardless, it is obvious that WATCHING a man do something is not the same as MAKING him do it.  Likewise, KNOWING that a man will accept Christ is not the same as MAKING that man do so.

If the lost are made to reject Christ, then one cannot condemn them for such rejection as they are never really able to choose to accept or reject Him.  As such, the lost's rejection of Christ would actually be obedience – not something worthy of judgement and/or damnation.  Such would not be justice.

God clearly made man with the ability to relate to and/or reject Him.  Each of us makes that choice and it is precisely that choice, and only that choice, that ultimately determines one's eternal habitation.  Isaiah 1:18 says,

Come now, and let us reason together, saith the LORD:
though your sins be as scarlet, they shall be as white as snow;
though they be red like crimson, they shall be as wool."

How does such solicited  reasoning  from God toward man figure into Calvinist sovereignal, "man has no choice" thinking?

If we are totally "elect" – that is created without choice – then all of God's efforts to win man serve no purpose other than, perhaps a play – something to fill and pass the time?  All the suffering in one's life, all the prayers offered for one's children and loved ones, would be in vain – a truly sad and tragic play.

If we are totally "elect" – created without choice – then all of God's efforts to win man serve no purpose other than, perhaps a play?
Being made in God's image and by growing in Him we come to understand and appreciate righteous judgement, mercy, and real love.  If we, as earthly parents, give our children "good things" – don't we suppose God does also – only more so?  We need not even "suppose" such a thing for God says in Matthew 7:11,
If ye then, being evil, know how to give good gifts unto your children,

God says "I have made you wonderfully.  I have given you the capacity even to NOT love me, to even rebel against and reject me in every fashion.  That is part of my love.  And yet, it is not my desire that any be lost."

More arguments can be raised and the debate continue, but it has already raged for hundreds of years now.  Calvinists are so insecure in their being "chosen" that they MUST preach it again and again and again.  Instead, let me conclude with this: In creating man with free will and even the ability to obey or disobey God (e.g. eating of "the tree of the knowledge of good and evil," Genesis 2:17), as well as the ability to accept or reject Christ (e.g. God's offer of Salvation), God seemingly  surrendered a portion of His sovereignty to give both men and angels free will.  He did this, I believe, because He knew that from a world of free creatures, even though  they would fall,  He remained powerful enough that He would still work out a deeper happiness,  greater joy,  and a fuller splendor in them,  than  any world of automatons would ever admit.
